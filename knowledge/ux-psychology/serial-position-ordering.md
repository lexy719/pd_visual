## serial-position-ordering
tags: psychology, serial-position, ordering, memory, narrative, layout

First and last are remembered; the middle is not. Order accordingly.

**Problem it solves** — section order is usually decided by internal logic (how the team thinks about the product), which buries the strongest material in the middle, where it is least likely to be recalled.
**When to use it** — deciding section sequence, nav order, list order, or which of several strong claims opens and closes the page.
**Structure** — Put the strongest claim first and the second-strongest last → place necessary-but-unpersuasive material (specs, detail, caveats) in the middle → close on the action you want remembered.
**Why it works** — recall is U-shaped: the opening sets the frame and the ending is what the reader leaves with. Middle material is read but rarely retained, which makes it the right home for completeness rather than persuasion.
**Expected visual outcome** — the page opens and closes strong; the middle reads as informative and denser, without competing for the emphasis the ends carry.
**Examples** — the best proof point in the closing section rather than third of six; the strongest differentiator in the hero rather than in a mid-page grid.
