## von-restorff-single-anchor
tags: psychology, von-restorff, isolation, emphasis, layout, color

The item that differs is the item that is remembered — so only one may differ.

**Problem it solves** — the isolation effect is powerful and self-cancelling. Highlight one card and it is remembered; highlight three and the highlight becomes the new baseline, so none of them stand out.
**When to use it** — a set of parallel items where exactly one should win: the recommended tier, the primary action, the featured case study.
**When NOT** — when the items genuinely are equal. Manufacturing a standout the content doesn't support reads as a sales trick and costs trust.
**Structure** — Keep every sibling visually identical → change exactly one property on the chosen item (accent background, or scale, or elevation — one, not three) → leave everything else untouched.
**Why it works** — attention is drawn by difference, not by intensity. One deviation in an otherwise uniform set is unmissable at any size; a second deviation halves the value of the first.
**Expected visual outcome** — the set reads as calm and uniform with one obvious winner, and the emphasis survives being viewed at a glance or at thumbnail scale.
**Examples** — one accent-filled pricing tier among plain ones; one featured item in an otherwise uniform grid.
