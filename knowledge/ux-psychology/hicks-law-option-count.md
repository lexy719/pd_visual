## hicks-law-option-count
tags: psychology, hicks-law, choice, conversion, layout

Decision time rises with the number and complexity of choices — so cut the choices.

**Problem it solves** — more options feel generous and convert worse. Every added tier, nav item or CTA taxes every visitor, including the ones who would have taken the first option.
**When to use it** — pricing tables, navigation, any section with more than a handful of parallel choices, and anywhere the drop-off happens at the moment of decision.
**Structure** — Reduce to 3-5 parallel options where possible → if more are genuinely needed, group them into named categories so the first decision is between 3 groups, not 12 items → mark one option as the default or recommended.
**Why it works** — the cost is per-decision, not per-page. Grouping turns one hard 12-way decision into two easy ones, and a recommended default lets the undecided majority opt out of deciding at all.
**Expected visual outcome** — sections read as decisive; the full set of choices can be taken in at a glance without scanning back and forth.
**Examples** — three pricing tiers with one marked recommended; a nav grouped into a few labelled areas rather than a flat list of twelve.
