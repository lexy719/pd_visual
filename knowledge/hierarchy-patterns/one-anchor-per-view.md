## one-anchor-per-view
tags: hierarchy, focal-point, emphasis, layout, typography

Each screenful gets exactly one element designed to be seen first.

**Problem it solves** — two co-equal focal points in one view force the reader to arbitrate. They usually resolve it by skipping both.
**When to use it** — every section, but especially dense ones: pricing, feature grids, anything with multiple calls to action.
**Structure** — Per section: name the single anchor (headline, product shot, or primary action) → give it the largest contrast jump in the view → let everything else support or explain it.
**Why it works** — attention is serial, not parallel. A view with one entry point is read; a view with two is scanned and abandoned. The anchor also tells the reader what the section is FOR before they read a word.
**Expected visual outcome** — each section has an obvious starting point, and the reader's path down the page feels decided rather than optional.
**Examples** — a pricing section anchored by the recommended tier; a feature block anchored by one headline claim rather than four equal cards.
