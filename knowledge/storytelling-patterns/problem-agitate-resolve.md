## problem-agitate-resolve
tags: narrative, storytelling, persuasion, conversion, layout

Name the reader's problem, make its cost concrete, then present the product as the resolution.

**Problem it solves** — a feature list assumes the reader already believes they have a problem. Most don't, so features answer a question nobody asked.
**When to use it** — the audience is unaware or half-aware of the pain, and the product only makes sense once the cost is felt. Strongest against a painful status quo.
**When NOT** — the audience is already shopping and knows exactly what they want; agitating a solved problem reads as condescending and wastes the fold.
**Structure** — Problem, stated plainly → Cost of the problem (concrete and specific) → Resolution (the product) → Proof → Action.
**Why it works** — a stated problem creates tension the product then discharges. Value is judged against a cost the reader has just conceded is real, so the same feature reads as relief rather than as a boast.
**Expected visual outcome** — the opening reads plain and serious rather than promotional, and the page visibly opens up — lighter, more spacious — at the resolution.
**Examples** — a developer tool opening on the outage it prevents; a service opening on the hours the current process burns.
