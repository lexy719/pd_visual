## origin-to-proof
tags: narrative, storytelling, origin, credibility, layout

Earn the claim before making it: open on where the thing came from, close on evidence that it works.

**Problem it solves** — a claim made before credibility exists reads as marketing. An origin gives the claim somewhere to stand.
**When to use it** — craft, provenance or expertise IS the differentiator: a roaster, a studio, a maker, a named specialist.
**When NOT** — a commodity purchase where origin is irrelevant to the decision; the reader wants price and availability, not a founding myth.
**Structure** — Origin (who, where, why) → Process (how it is actually made) → The product → Proof (results, testimony) → Action.
**Why it works** — provenance converts a claim into a consequence. By the time proof arrives the reader already has a reason to believe it, so the evidence confirms rather than argues.
**Expected visual outcome** — the page reads as editorial rather than transactional; earlier sections carry more photographic weight, later ones more text and evidence.
**Examples** — a single-origin coffee brand, an independent design studio, a practice led by a named specialist.
