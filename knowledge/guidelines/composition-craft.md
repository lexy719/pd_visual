# Composition craft — how to make a page look designed

Most design rules are constraints: contrast floors, spacing minimums, things not to do. Following all
of them produces a page that is correct and flat. These entries are the opposite kind of knowledge —
the positive devices that make a page read as composed rather than assembled. Reach for them
deliberately; two or three used well beat all ten used at once.

## Depth comes from occlusion, not shadow

tags: composition, depth, layering, overlap, craft, universal

Layering reads as real depth only when one element OVERLAPS and hides part of another — occlusion is
the signal the eye trusts, while a drop shadow on an otherwise flat box reads as a sticker. Three
reliable devices: let an image cross a section boundary so it sits over both bands; pull a card up
over the block above it (negative margin of 40–80px with a z-index step); set type over the edge of
media so the letterforms interrupt the picture. Each needs a deliberate stacking order and a small
overlap — 8–15% of the element is enough. A page of non-overlapping rectangles with shadows added is
still a flat page.

## Hierarchy is built from four contrasts, not just size

tags: composition, hierarchy, contrast, emphasis, craft, universal

When everything is distinguished only by font size, a page flattens into a list; real hierarchy comes
from combining SIZE with three other contrasts — weight, isolation, and position. Isolation is the
most underused: an element with twice the surrounding space of its neighbours reads as important
regardless of its size. Position matters because the eye assigns significance to what breaks the
established alignment, not to what sits obediently in the column. Weight and colour do the fine
tuning. The practical test: convert the design to greyscale and squint — if only one thing survives
as dominant, the hierarchy is working.

## Break the grid on purpose, once

tags: composition, grid, tension, asymmetry, craft, editorial

A grid that is never broken produces order without interest, so choose exactly ONE element per page
to violate it deliberately — a pull-quote extending into the margin, an image starting left of the
container edge, a numeral hanging outside the text column. The break must be large enough to read as
intentional (at least 15–20% beyond the boundary; a 4px misalignment reads as an error) and
everything else must stay strictly aligned, because a break only registers as tension against
visible discipline. Two or three breaks and the grid stops existing, which returns the page to
noise.

## One scale jump carries more than many gradual steps

tags: composition, scale, typography, drama, craft, hierarchy

Pages feel timid when every element is a slightly different size; drama comes from ONE violent jump —
a headline or numeral 6–10× the body size — with everything else held quiet and close together. The
jump works because of what surrounds it: an oversized element among other large elements is just
noise. Use it on the single thing the visitor should remember (the number, the claim, the name), and
resist scaling anything else up to match. This is the cheapest way to make a page feel confident, and
the first thing missing from pages that read as flat.

## Bleed to an edge to escape the box

tags: composition, bleed, full-bleed, edge, media, craft

A page composed entirely of contained, padded blocks feels like a document; letting ONE element run
to the viewport edge makes the page feel like a designed surface. Candidates: a full-bleed image band
that breaks the container rhythm, a colour field extending past the content column, media that runs
off one side while its caption stays in the grid. The device depends on contrast — it only works if
the rest of the page respects the container. Bleeding everything produces a slideshow with no
structure, which reads as chaotic rather than confident.

## Pace the page: alternate dense and open

tags: composition, pacing, rhythm, density, craft, editorial

A page where every section has the same density is monotonous regardless of individual section
quality, so pace it deliberately: follow a dense, information-rich block with an open, quiet one.
Rhythm is what makes long pages readable — the quiet sections give the dense ones weight, and the
dense ones make the quiet ones feel earned rather than empty. Plan this at page level, not section by
section, since the effect only exists in the sequence. A useful shape: open strong, tighten through
the middle where the substance lives, release near the close.

## Every page needs one anchor moment

tags: composition, anchor, memorability, focus, craft, universal

A page the visitor can describe afterwards has ONE moment engineered to be memorable — an
unforgettable image, a single enormous number, a sentence set larger than seems reasonable, one
unexpected interaction — and everything else deliberately supports it. Without a designated anchor,
attention distributes evenly and nothing is retained. Choose the anchor before laying out the page,
put the run's strongest asset into it, and protect it by keeping neighbouring sections quieter than
they want to be. Competing anchors cancel: two spectacular moments produce a page with none.

## Type can be the image

tags: composition, typography, type-as-image, craft, editorial, minimal

When photography is weak, unavailable, or off-brand, set type large enough to become the visual — a
headline occupying half the viewport is imagery, not text. This is the professional answer to a
missing photo budget and it outperforms generic stock or mediocre generated pictures every time.
It requires real commitment: at display sizes tighten tracking (-0.02 to -0.04em), drop line-height
toward 0.95–1.05, and let the block sit against generous emptiness. Half-hearted large type just
looks like an oversized paragraph; the device only works when the type is unmistakably the subject.

## Craft is visible in the details that repeat

tags: composition, detail, craft, consistency, polish, universal

A page reads as professionally made when its small decisions are consistent: one corner radius
throughout, one border weight and colour, one shadow depth, one icon style, aligned optical edges.
Viewers cannot articulate these but reliably perceive their absence as cheapness. The common failures
are mixing radii (rounded cards beside square images), several border greys, icons from different
families, and text that aligns to a container rather than optically to neighbouring text. Choose each
value once and repeat it without exception — consistency in the small things buys more perceived
quality than any single flourish.

## Give surfaces material, not just colour

tags: composition, texture, material, surface, craft, depth

Flat colour fields are what make generated pages feel thin, so give at least one surface real
material: a subtle grain or noise, a paper or fabric texture behind a quiet section, a duotone
treatment that unifies imagery, a hairline rule system that suggests print. The effect should be felt
rather than noticed — texture strong enough to identify is usually too strong. This matters most for
brands with tactile subject matter (craft, food, print, physical objects), where an untextured page
contradicts the thing it is describing.
