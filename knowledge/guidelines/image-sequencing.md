# Image sequencing — how a page's images relate to each other

A page's images are a sequence, not a collection. These rules describe what a deliberately staged
set looks like; they are the grounding for the page-level shot plan, decided once per run.

## Sequence syntax: an image earns its meaning from its neighbours

tags: image, sequence, media, editorial, rhythm, universal

A photograph acquires a different value depending on the image before and after it — the transition
is where the meaning lives, exactly as a cut carries meaning in film. Adjacent images must be
deliberately related: either continuity (same world, next beat) or pointed contrast (high noon
against twilight, crowd against solitude). An image chosen only for its own topic, blind to its
neighbours, reads as randomly assembled even when it is individually good. The pair is the unit of
composition; three related images open a phrase.

## Scale rhythm: alternate establishing, medium, and detail

tags: image, sequence, scale, rhythm, media, pacing, universal

Wide shots establish the world, medium shots build connection, close-ups prove detail and create
intimacy — and the rhythm between them is the page's visual tempo. Two same-scale images next to
each other kill that tempo; a page of all-medium shots is monotone, a page of all-detail shots is
claustrophobic. One establishing shot opens the world and is not repeated: once the reader has been
placed, re-establishing reads as starting over.

## Aesthetic constancy: unpredictable content, one constant world

tags: image, media, consistency, light, lens, premium, universal

What holds a strong sequence together is not repeated subject matter but a constant aesthetic —
one light quality, one lens language, one finish, across every image, while the content varies
freely. A sequence of individually weaker images with one voice beats a set of individually
stronger images with none. Mixing visual registers — stock photography beside generated imagery,
studio light beside daylight, crisp beside grainy — is the single fastest way to make a page feel
assembled from parts, because the aesthetic seam is visible even when every image is on-topic.

## One dominant image per page

tags: image, hierarchy, media, editorial, layout, dominant

Every spread has exactly one dominant image, noticeably larger than the rest; everything else
supports it. Scale contrast is how imagery communicates priority — two competing hero images cancel
each other, and a page where every image is the same size says nothing is important. The dominant
image carries the page's one indispensable visual statement; if it were the only image that loaded,
the page should still make its point.

## Subject continuity: the product is the same object in every shot

tags: image, media, product, consistency, brand, continuity

When a brand has one physical subject — a bottle, a garment, a machine — it must be visibly the
SAME object in every appearance: same proportions, same label, same finish. A subject that mutates
between shots destroys credibility faster than any low-quality render, because the reader registers
the inconsistency as dishonesty rather than as a style choice. This requires the subject's identity
to be fixed once, described identically everywhere it appears; imagery sourced shot-by-shot from
keyword search cannot hold a subject constant and is disqualified for subject-led pages.

## The colour script: stage all imagery once, before any image exists

tags: image, media, art-direction, sequence, planning, universal

Film production decides the visual arc of every scene in one pass — a colour script — before a
single frame is rendered; each shot then answers to the map instead of being invented in isolation.
The page-level equivalent: the full image sequence (which scale, which job, which light) is decided
as one artefact, once, and every individual image request derives from it. Per-image decisions made
independently cannot produce a sequence, no matter how good each decision is.
