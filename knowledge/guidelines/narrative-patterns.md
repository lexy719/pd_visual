# Narrative and scroll-story patterns

Each pattern defines a communication sequence, not a layout template. Retrieve the pattern for its reasoning, then compose a page around the brief rather than reproducing its exact sections.

## Product proof: show the change before the claim

tags: minimal, technical, trustworthy, product, saas, layout, narrative, proof

For a tool or service, open with the specific job it makes easier. Follow with a concrete state change: before/after, input/output, or a workflow transition. Only then add features and proof. This makes the visitor understand the value before asking them to interpret a feature grid. Motion can reveal state change, but the core message must still work as a static sequence.

## Editorial launch: control the reveal

tags: premium, editorial, luxury, layout, narrative, reveal, motion

An editorial launch should behave like a paced argument: establish one strong visual thesis, introduce the object or idea in detail, explain the material or process behind it, then widen into proof and action. Hold back information intentionally, but never the basic offer. Use a small number of large transitions rather than animating every section; the pauses are part of the luxury.

## Cinematic scroll story: each chapter earns its pin

tags: premium, playful, aggressive, video, scroll, layout, narrative, motion

Use a pinned or full-bleed scene only when it communicates a genuine chapter: scale, transformation, craft, or consequence. Every chapter needs a sentence-length takeaway, an entry state, an exit state, and a static mobile interpretation. A sequence of unrelated spectacle is not a story; it makes visitors work to discover what is being sold.

## Trust journey: answer risk in the order it appears

tags: trustworthy, finance, corporate, healthcare, layout, narrative, proof

For consequential products, lead with the problem and outcome, then show credibility, process, safeguards, and only then the conversion action. Testimonials belong after the visitor knows what is being evaluated. Use visual calm and predictable navigation to make the information feel dependable; novelty should not become another risk the visitor has to assess.

## Culture brand: turn identity into participation

tags: playful, aggressive, lifestyle, consumer, layout, narrative, community

For a culture-led product, start with an attitude or world the audience wants to join, then connect that energy to tangible product proof. Repeated symbols, language, and physical metaphors can build recognition across the page. Keep one stable informational rail—price, product access, or navigation—so expressive moments do not make buying difficult.
