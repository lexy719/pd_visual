## asymmetry-with-intent
tags: layout, asymmetry, composition, editorial, balance

An off-centre composition must be balanced by weight, or it just looks broken.

**Problem it solves** — centring everything is safe and inert; asymmetry is where energy comes from. But asymmetry without counterweight reads as a mistake rather than a choice.
**When to use it** — editorial, premium and brand-led work that must not look templated; a hero where one side carries the argument and the other carries the image.
**When NOT** — dense functional interfaces (dashboards, tables, forms), where predictability beats energy.
**Structure** — Commit to an unequal split (roughly 60/40 or 70/30, not 55/45) → counterweight the lighter side with something dense: a bold headline, a dark block, a tight text group → keep one shared alignment edge so the imbalance reads as deliberate.
**Why it works** — the eye accepts imbalance when the composition still resolves. A near-equal split reads as a failed centre; a decisive split reads as a decision, and the shared edge is what proves it was one.
**Expected visual outcome** — the section feels composed and energetic rather than centred-by-default, and still resolves — nothing looks like it slid out of place.
**Examples** — a hero with a 60/40 text-to-image split sharing a baseline; a feature row where the heavy image and the dense text block trade sides down the page.
